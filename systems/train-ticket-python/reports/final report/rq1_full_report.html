Comparing RL-Based and Non-RL Adaptive Tracing Methods in a Microservice System

Research Question: How does RL-based adaptive tracing compare to non-RL adaptive tracing baselines, such as rule-based and optimization/sampling-based methods?

q_learning sarsa bandit rule kmeans
Testbed: Train-Ticket (FastAPI + Docker Compose) Tracing: OpenTelemetry + Jaeger Scenarios: healthy, faulted

Motivation

Adaptive tracing is used to control tracing overhead while still keeping useful runtime visibility in distributed systems. The literature shows many tracing and sampling approaches, but direct comparison between RL-based adaptive tracing and non-RL baselines is still limited. Because of that, it is not yet clear whether RL actually provides a practical advantage over simpler adaptive approaches.

This experiment was designed to address that gap by comparing RL-based methods and non-RL baselines under the same microservice testbed, workload, and runtime scenarios.

Literature Gap Addressed

This report mainly addresses the following gap from the literature review:

  • Comparison with non-RL adaptive tracing methods is limited.

It also partially relates to the gap that behavior under changing runtime conditions is not explored enough, since the experiment includes both healthy and faulted conditions.

Objective

The objective of this experiment is to compare the behavior of RL-based and non-RL adaptive tracing methods in terms of tracing overhead and runtime response under the same conditions.

Methods Compared

RL-based methods:

  • q_learning
  • sarsa
  • bandit

Non-RL baselines:

  • rule
  • kmeans

Experimental Setup

The experiment was performed on the Train-Ticket Python microservice testbed with OpenTelemetry-based tracing and a sampling agent that adjusts tracing behavior at runtime.

The same workload and tracing setup were used for all methods. Two scenarios were tested:

  • Healthy condition: ORDER_DELAY_MS=50, ORDER_ERROR_RATE=0.01, TRAVEL_DELAY_MS=30, TRAVEL_ERROR_RATE=0.0
  • Faulted condition: ORDER_DELAY_MS=200, ORDER_ERROR_RATE=0.08, TRAVEL_DELAY_MS=120, TRAVEL_ERROR_RATE=0.03

Each method was run under both conditions, and metrics were collected from the adaptive tracing status and summary endpoints.

Metrics Used

  • average reward
  • average sampling rate
  • minimum and maximum sampling rate
  • final latency
  • final QPS
  • final error rate

For RL methods, average reward is available directly and reflects the current reward design. For non-RL methods, reward is not produced, so the comparison relies mainly on sampling rate, latency, throughput, and general behavior.

Interpretation of Results

Overall comparison

The results show that RL-based adaptive tracing performed competitively against the tested non-RL baselines. Among the RL methods, q_learning performed best in both healthy and faulted scenarios. It maintained low tracing rates while achieving the strongest reward values in the RL group.

Healthy condition behavior

Under healthy conditions, the system remained stable and the methods generally kept tracing activity low. This is expected, because when the system is healthy, increasing tracing too much mostly adds overhead without much additional benefit.

  • q_learning and bandit both stayed at the minimum tracing rate (0.05), which suggests conservative and efficient behavior.
  • Among the RL methods, q_learning achieved the highest average reward, meaning it balanced the reward terms more effectively than the other RL methods.
  • sarsa explored more than the other RL methods and reached higher tracing rates, but that extra tracing did not improve its overall reward.
  • rule also stayed at low tracing and behaved simply and stably.
  • kmeans remained at a fixed 0.10, which is still moderate, but less conservative than q_learning, bandit, and rule.

In this scenario, the strongest result is that q_learning achieved the best RL outcome while keeping tracing at the minimum level.

Faulted condition behavior

Under faulted conditions, latency increased and throughput dropped. This created a more difficult environment for adaptive tracing and made the comparison more meaningful.

  • q_learning again produced the strongest RL result. It slightly increased its average tracing rate from 0.0500 to 0.0545, suggesting some adaptation without aggressive overhead.
  • bandit stayed conservative at 0.05, which kept overhead low, but it did not match q_learning in overall reward.
  • sarsa increased tracing much more aggressively, with an average rate of 0.1182 and a maximum of 0.80, but this did not improve reward.
  • rule stayed simple and low-cost, but its final QPS in the faulted case was much lower than the other methods.
  • kmeans kept a fixed 0.10 and showed the highest final latency among the compared methods in the faulted batch.

In this scenario, q_learning again appears to be the strongest method because it adapts slightly while still keeping tracing overhead controlled.

RL vs non-RL takeaway

This experiment suggests that RL-based adaptive tracing, especially q_learning, can perform at least as well as the tested non-RL baselines and may perform better under degraded runtime conditions. The non-RL methods were simpler and stable, but they did not show a stronger overall result in this comparison.

Answer to RQ1

Based on the experimental results, RL-based adaptive tracing performed competitively against the tested non-RL baselines, and q_learning produced the strongest overall behavior in both healthy and faulted scenarios. It maintained low tracing rates while achieving the highest reward among the RL methods and better overall adaptation than the tested rule and kmeans baselines. This suggests that RL-based adaptive tracing can offer a practical advantage over simpler adaptive tracing methods, at least in the tested settings.

What Gap Does This Experiment Cover?

This experiment directly covers the literature gap that direct comparison between RL-based and non-RL adaptive tracing methods is limited.

It covers that gap because:

  • it evaluates RL methods and non-RL baselines in the same environment
  • it uses the same workload and the same testbed
  • it compares them under both healthy and faulted conditions
  • it provides side-by-side metrics instead of evaluating one method in isolation

Limitations

  1. Error rate remained zero. Even in the faulted condition, the final measured order-service error rate remained 0.0. This means the faulted scenario mainly reflects latency and throughput degradation rather than persistent request failures.
  2. Non-RL methods do not expose reward values. rule and kmeans do not provide reward outputs, so comparison with RL methods is not perfectly symmetric.
  3. Only two non-RL baselines were tested. A stronger optimization-based or literature-derived baseline would improve the comparison.
  4. Single testbed and short runs. The experiments were performed on one microservice testbed with relatively short runs.
  5. Trace usefulness was not measured directly. The comparison uses runtime and tracing-control metrics, but it does not yet directly measure diagnostic value or anomaly capture quality.

Discussion

This report shows that the first research question can be answered meaningfully with the current testbed. The result is not that RL is always better in every possible case, but that RL-based adaptive tracing, particularly q_learning, showed better overall behavior than the tested non-RL baselines in this setup.

This is important because adaptive tracing research often lacks direct side-by-side comparison between RL and simpler baselines. The experiment therefore contributes practical evidence to that discussion.

At the same time, the limitations show what should be improved in later work: stronger fault scenarios, broader non-RL baselines, longer experiments, and direct measures of trace usefulness.

Conclusion

This experiment answered the first research question by comparing RL-based adaptive tracing with non-RL baselines under healthy and faulted conditions. Among the tested methods, q_learning produced the strongest overall result, combining low tracing rates with the highest RL reward and stable behavior across both scenarios. bandit remained conservative and performed reasonably well, while sarsa explored more aggressively without improving results. The non-RL baselines, rule and kmeans, were stable but did not outperform q_learning.

Overall, the results provide initial evidence that RL-based adaptive tracing can be more effective than the tested non-RL adaptive tracing methods, especially when the system is under degraded runtime conditions. This directly addresses an identified gap in the literature and provides a strong starting point for the next research questions.
