Cross-system RQ1 synthesis

Final RQ1 Report Across Train-Ticket, Timescale OpenTelemetry Demo, and Spring Petclinic Microservices

This report answers RQ1 across all three applications used in the project: Train-Ticket, Timescale OpenTelemetry Demo, and Spring Petclinic Microservices. The question is whether RL-based adaptive tracing performs better than non-RL adaptive tracing when both are evaluated under the same runtime conditions inside the same system. The combined result is not a single universal winner. Instead, the evidence shows that RL methods are consistently competitive, but the strongest method depends on the application.

1. Research Question

RQ1: How does RL-based adaptive tracing compare to non-RL adaptive tracing baselines, such as rule-based and clustering-based methods, when all methods are evaluated under the same microservice system and the same runtime conditions?

The key point is not whether one algorithm wins a benchmark once. The point is whether RL methods remain advantageous or at least competitive when the same comparison is repeated on three different microservice applications with different service topologies, traffic behavior, telemetry backends, and fault behavior.

2. Terms and Methods

Adaptive tracing

Adaptive tracing changes the trace sampling rate at runtime instead of leaving tracing permanently fixed. A higher rate captures more traces but increases overhead. A lower rate reduces overhead but may miss important diagnostic information.

Healthy vs faulted

Healthy means the application runs under normal conditions. Faulted means the workload or service behavior is intentionally degraded so that the tracing controller is tested under stress rather than only in steady state.

Measured outputs

The core runtime signals used across the reports are latency, QPS (throughput), error rate, trace total, and, for RL methods, average reward. Reward is useful, but it is not always the strongest indicator of system quality.

Q-Learning

An off-policy temporal-difference RL method. It learns the long-term value of each tracing action for each observed state, even while exploring. In this study it is the strongest method on Train-Ticket.

SARSA

An on-policy temporal-difference RL method. It updates using the action that the current policy actually chooses next and, in this project, proved to be the most exploratory RL method overall.

Bandit

A simpler RL-style action-value method without explicit state-transition learning. It focuses on the immediate payoff of tracing actions and is the strongest Petclinic policy.

Rule

A non-RL baseline with hand-crafted thresholds. It reacts to observed runtime signals directly and is the strongest Timescale latency baseline.

K-Means

A clustering-based non-RL baseline. It groups observed runtime states and maps each cluster to a tracing rate, providing a simple data-driven alternative to hand-written rules.

3. The Three Applications

Train-Ticket

Train-Ticket

A railway ticket booking microservice benchmark with richer service interactions and the strongest RQ1 evidence set. It acts as the largest and most mature application in the study.

Python microservices, adaptive tracing controller, experiment runner, healthy and faulted scenarios.

Healthy latency winnerBandit
Faulted latency winnerQ-Learning
Healthy reward leaderQ-Learning
Faulted reward leaderQ-Learning

Q-Learning is the strongest overall method in this application. It keeps tracing rates low while leading the RL group in reward and performing strongly under degraded conditions.

Evidence quality: Most stable result set of the three applications: longer runs, higher throughput, and many decision windows.

Timescale Demo

Timescale OpenTelemetry Demo

A lightweight password-generator microservice application where a generator fans out to upper, lower, digit, and special services. It is smaller than Train-Ticket but still produces genuine distributed traces.

OpenTelemetry Collector, Promscale, TimescaleDB, Jaeger, Grafana.

Healthy latency winnerRule
Faulted latency winnerRule
Healthy reward leaderSARSA
Faulted reward leaderQ-Learning

The rule baseline gives the best final latency in both healthy and faulted runs. RL remains competitive, but there is no single RL method that clearly dominates the final runtime metrics.

Evidence quality: Useful second testbed, but its reward-function caveat matters: higher reward does not always mean better runtime behavior.

Petclinic

Spring Petclinic Microservices

A realistic Spring Cloud business application with an API gateway, customers, visits, and vets services, plus configuration and discovery infrastructure.

Spring Cloud, Zipkin, Prometheus, gateway-driven traffic; faulted runs fall back to heavier degraded load when Chaos Monkey endpoints are unavailable.

Healthy latency winnerBandit
Faulted latency winnerBandit
Healthy reward leaderBandit
Faulted reward leaderBandit

Bandit is the strongest Petclinic policy on final runtime metrics, giving the best final latency and throughput in both healthy and faulted runs.

Evidence quality: Complete but weaker than the other two: runs are short, most policies have only 1-2 decision windows, and a few windows are empty.

7. Integrated Interpretation

Main synthesis

RL-based adaptive tracing is competitive across all three applications, but it is not universally superior in exactly the same way on every system. The strongest policy changes with the application. This matters because it means RQ1 cannot be answered credibly from only one benchmark.

Train-Ticket favors Q-Learning, the Timescale application favors the Rule baseline on final latency, and Petclinic favors Bandit on final runtime metrics.

What the three systems show together

  • Train-Ticket: strongest evidence for Q-Learning. RL methods are clearly competitive and Q-Learning is the most convincing overall winner.
  • Timescale Demo: no universal RL advantage. Rule gives the lowest latency, which shows that a simple heuristic can still beat RL on some systems.
  • Petclinic: Bandit outperforms the other policies on final latency and QPS, showing that a simpler RL method can be better than both deeper RL and non-RL baselines on a different application.

How reward should be interpreted

Reward is part of the reported output for RL methods, but it is not perfectly comparable across all three systems. In the Timescale application and Petclinic, reward design and runtime sparsity weaken its interpretability. For that reason, this final synthesis treats latency, QPS, error rate, and trace totals as the primary evidence and uses reward as a supporting signal rather than the sole criterion.

Evidence strength is not equal across the systems

The three applications do not contribute equally strong evidence. Train-Ticket is the most stable and mature result set. The Timescale application is valid but has a reward-design caveat. Petclinic is complete, but its policy runs are short and some windows are sparse. That does not invalidate Petclinic, but it does mean the strongest claims should still lean on Train-Ticket and then be checked against the other two systems.

8. Answer to RQ1 Across All Three Systems

Across Train-Ticket, Timescale OpenTelemetry Demo, and Spring Petclinic Microservices, RL-based adaptive tracing performs competitively against non-RL baselines, but there is no single method that wins every application. Q-Learning is the strongest overall method on Train-Ticket, Rule is the best final-latency method on the Timescale application, and Bandit is the strongest Petclinic policy on final runtime metrics. The combined answer to RQ1 is therefore:

RL-based adaptive tracing is a credible and often strong choice, but its advantage is system-dependent rather than universal. The results support the value of RL, yet they also show that simple non-RL baselines can still win on some systems. This is precisely why direct multi-system comparison is necessary.

10. Limitations and Next Steps

  • Reward symmetry is imperfect. Non-RL methods do not expose reward in the same way as RL methods, and some system-specific reward definitions are not pure performance scores.
  • Run quality varies by system. Train-Ticket is the strongest evidence set; Timescale and Petclinic are valid but less stable.
  • Fault models differ across applications. That is acceptable for RQ1, but it means the study compares methods under each application's realistic degraded condition rather than forcing one identical fault model onto all systems.
  • Longer repeated trials would strengthen the final comparison. That is especially true for Petclinic, where decision counts are low.

The logical next step after this report is a higher-level synthesis of what these results imply for later research questions, especially those about runtime changes, policy robustness, and adaptive tracing behavior under more varied degradation patterns.
